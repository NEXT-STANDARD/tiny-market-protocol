# Dawn: A Mosaic of 2026–2030

*A Speculative Chronicle of the TINY / AGORA Transition*

---

> The following vignettes are not predictions.
>
> They are plausible dispatches from a four-year transition, imagined
> forward from 2026. They are written in present tense on purpose: not
> because the authors believe these events *will* happen, but because
> the texture of how they *could* happen is more instructive than any
> clean forecast.
>
> Real names appear. Statements attributed to real individuals are
> fictional. Companies named below do not exist. Governments named below
> do. The forces, the patterns, and the underlying data are drawn from
> the research in `../research/sources.md`.
>
> Every vignette could be wrong in its specifics and still be right
> about the shape of what is coming.

## March 2026 — Tallinn

**"The Two-Person Unicorn"**

Dispatch from *The Information*, March 4 2026:

> Kalev Saar, 25, and Helena Kask, 26, confirmed on Wednesday that their
> eighteen-month-old legaltech company, Brevi, has closed a $1.2 billion
> tender offer led by an unnamed Gulf sovereign fund.
>
> Brevi employs two full-time people. Saar writes code. Kask handles
> everything else. The company's contract-review product runs on a
> customized stack of AI agents and currently serves 14,000 law firms
> across 43 countries.
>
> "We thought about raising a Series A," Saar said. "Then we realized
> we'd already made more money than the Series A would have given us. So
> we stopped."
>
> Asked whether this counts as the "one-person unicorn" that OpenAI's Sam
> Altman and Anthropic's Dario Amodei have publicly speculated about,
> Saar was curt: "We are two. You do the math."

On Hacker News the same day, the top comment reads:
*"Two founders isn't a one-person unicorn. But the point has been made."*

## September 2026 — GitHub

**"A Seed Goes Viral"**

On September 8 2026, the repository `NEXT-STANDARD/tiny-market-protocol`
crosses 10,000 stars. The README is unchanged from its original
CC0-dedicated form.

By late September:

- 47 forks exist.
- AIP-011 through AIP-028 have been proposed by contributors from
  twelve countries.
- A Korean working group publishes a largely compatible alternative
  called TIP (Tiny-market Interoperability Protocol).
- Balaji Srinivasan devotes an hour-long interview to the repository's
  implications.
- Marc Andreessen, asked about it on a podcast, says "it's cute" and
  changes the subject.
- Paul Graham writes a short essay titled *Solving the Wrong Problem*,
  arguing that founders are overthinking liquidity. The essay is forked
  as AIP-031 ("Rebuttal") within a week.

The original seed author, who signs only as @NEXT-STANDARD, has not
posted on X since the launch.

## January 2027 — Dubai International Financial Centre

**"The First Market"**

At 10:04 AM GST on January 19 2027, the DIFC TINY Pilot Exchange opens
trading. Twelve initial listings. Combined market capitalization at
opening: $3.2 billion.

The largest listing is *Nuxt Labs Holdings*, an open-source SaaS
collective incorporated in the DIFC. Six operators. $21M TTM revenue.
$7.4M net income.

The smallest is *Kargo Bakery Systems*, an Addis Ababa-based
pastry-logistics company serving 340 hotels across East Africa.
Founder and sole operator: Amira Bekele. $680K TTM revenue. $180K net
income.

Both trade on the same order book.

The opening "bell" is a recorded *adhan*-style tone, chosen instead of
the American ritual of ringing a physical bell — a ritual, the market
operator writes, "ill-suited to a market that will never close."

DIFC TINY is open 24/7. In its first month, 61% of trading volume is
executed by agents on behalf of retail principals.

## February 2028 — Fukuoka, Tenjin District

**"Opening Bell"**

The Fukuoka TINY Market opens at 09:00 JST on February 14 2028.

There is no physical bell. Instead, at the moment of opening, a
synthesized voice reads a single sentence in the trading hall:

> *小さきもののために市を開く。*
>
> "A market opened for small things."

Forty-seven initial listings. Nineteen countries of origin. Fifteen are
single-founder companies. Three are cooperatives. One is operated by 34
part-time volunteers under a single named accountability sponsor.

Opening day combined market capitalization: ¥1.2 trillion
(approximately $8.1B).

The first trade executes 40 milliseconds after opening. A buy order
for ¥430,000 worth of *Mecheri Textiles*, a seven-person Moroccan
fabric-weaving company with $1.2M TTM revenue. The buyer is an agent
operated by a Singaporean pension fund, executing a mandate that
specifies "textile businesses with operational continuity predating
1960 and verifiable fair-labor practices."

The seller is another agent, operated on behalf of an individual in
Nagoya whose intent reads, in part: *"Reduce concentration in African
consumer goods upon 30%+ appreciation."*

No human places an order on opening day.

## August 2028 — Brussels

**"Agent Market Directive"**

The European Commission publishes Directive 2028/AI-MKT-1, the *Agent
Market Directive*. Key provisions:

- Any agent-mediated trading venue serving EU persons MUST register
  with ESMA.
- Every agent mandate involving EU principals MUST be available in
  both machine-readable and natural-language form — *Dual Form*,
  lifted verbatim from AIP-003.
- Fault-attribution MUST follow the AIP-002 §3.2 cascade, backed for
  residual cases by an EU reinsurance pool.
- Non-compliant venues will be blocked from EU access within 18 months.

The Commission cites the open-source *tiny-market-protocol* repository
by URL in Annex II. An unnamed commissioner, off the record, calls it
"the most influential piece of infrastructure legislation Europe has
imported in a decade."

A16z partner Katherine Boyle tweets (fictional):
*"The EU is the only actor in this whole story that understood what
was happening in time."*

## November 2028 — Leaked Internal Memo

**"The a16z Response"**

From a memo (fictional) circulated among Andreessen Horowitz general
partners, November 11 2028; leaked to *The Information* in December:

> Our 2021 through 2024 cohorts will deliver DPI below our required
> threshold. This is already priced into our next LP conversation.
>
> The harder question is the 2025+ cohort. TINY listings in Dubai and
> Fukuoka are absorbing the profile of company that previously entered
> our Series A through C. These companies — profitable, 10-to-50
> person, founder-owned — never enter our deal flow now. They go public
> at $50M of revenue instead of raising $30M to maybe get there.
>
> We have three options:
>
> 1. **Compete:** Open our own agent-native market. This requires
>    regulatory relationships we do not have.
> 2. **Complement:** Reposition as growth capital for companies
>    post-TINY listing that want to scale beyond ten employees.
> 3. **Retreat:** Focus exclusively on frontier-capital domains (bio,
>    fusion, semis, space) where the TINY model does not apply.
>
> Option 2 is probably correct but requires rebuilding our team.
> Option 3 is our default. Option 1 is where the next fortune gets
> made.

The memo leaks in December. Within 48 hours, three a16z partners
announce departures.

## September 2029 — The First Scandal

**"When an Agent Lies"**

From *Nikkei Asia*, September 9 2029 (fictional):

> A mid-sized TINY listing suspended trading yesterday after an
> independent audit revealed that the company's Real-Time Company Data
> Feed had been fabricating customer-count data for at least four
> months.
>
> Unusually, the fraud was not detected by a regulator. It was
> detected by a competing agent, operating under a pension fund's
> mandate that specified "flag outlier growth patterns for review."
> The agent filed a formal Challenge under AIP-004's Observation
> Window provisions and triggered the audit automatically.
>
> The company's sole founder, who had personally signed every CDF
> snapshot under AIP-002 identity rules, faces delisting and
> jurisdictional criminal charges.
>
> Critics say this proves the TINY model is vulnerable to fraud.
> Supporters counter that the fraud was caught in four months — an
> order of magnitude faster than Enron or Wirecard.

The repository receives its first meaningful volume of pull requests
hardening the CDF integrity spec within the week.

## November 2029 — Retrospective

**"A Postmortem"**

From a long-form essay in *Nikkei Asia*, November 30 2029, by an
unnamed market historian (fictional):

> We were promised, in 2007 and again in 2017, that the internet would
> democratize access to capital markets. Both times, what we actually
> got was more efficient execution of the same basic transaction:
> human buys, human sells, same kinds of humans on both sides.
>
> What happened between 2026 and 2029 was different. For the first
> time, the identities on both sides of a trade became partially
> non-human. And the identities that mattered most — the *principals*,
> the ones whose interests were being served — became *smaller*. A
> retirement account in Yokohama. A seven-person bakery supplier in
> Ethiopia. A student in Buenos Aires with $3,000.
>
> Nobody planned this, exactly. A group of regulators in Fukuoka
> copied notes from a meeting in Tallinn. An anonymous seed author
> published an open-source protocol specification and left it to the
> wind. A Moroccan weaver listed his company because his cousin told
> him he could.
>
> The historians of the 2030s will probably write that this was
> inevitable. It was not inevitable. Any of a dozen things could have
> stopped it. The one that did not stop it, in the end, was that the
> old system was no longer serving the people it was built for.
>
> When an old system no longer serves the people it was built for,
> and when the replacement is technically cheaper, philosophically
> simpler, and open to anyone — the replacement wins. Eventually.
> Messily. And without the permission of the old system's
> beneficiaries.

## End Frame — 2030

**"What Comes Next"**

These vignettes end in 2030. They should not be read as a forecast of
what 2030 will look like. They should be read as a plausible account
of *how* 2030 could come to look the way it will.

The details will differ. Fukuoka may not be the center. Brevi may be
called something else, headquartered somewhere else. The a16z memo may
never leak. The SEC may react differently. The Japanese Diet transcript
may read more favorably, or not at all.

What these vignettes try to capture is not the specifics but the
*shape* — the way an old order becomes visibly inadequate before anyone
has named its replacement, the way specific individuals in specific
cities make specific decisions that aggregate into a transition, and
the way the formal documents catch up to the facts on the ground only
after the facts have already changed.

The AIP specifications in this repository are the formal documents.

The scenarios in this file are the facts on the ground, imagined
forward.

The gap between them is where the actual future gets built.
