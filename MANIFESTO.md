# MANIFESTO

*The existing capital stack was designed for a species that is going extinct.*

---

## The Old World

For the last fifty years, the path to success for ambitious companies was a single
tunnel with a fixed shape:

> **Seed → Series A/B/C/D → IPO → Acquisition or Perpetual Public Life**

The tunnel was designed for a specific kind of creature: a company that would grow
from 10 people to 10,000, hire salespeople to cover geographies, build legal
departments to manage contracts, and eventually need hundreds of millions of
dollars simply to **coordinate the humans** it employed.

Venture capital existed to underwrite that coordination cost.
The IPO existed to liquidate it.

Both assumptions are now breaking.

---

## The New Species

A new kind of company has appeared in the last 24 months:

- **Midjourney** reached $500M ARR with ~40 people and zero external funding.
- **Cursor** reached $1B ARR within months, with a team that fits in a conference room.
- **Base44** was sold for $80M cash six months after founding — by a single person.
- **Gumloop** targets unicorn valuation with a team of ten.

These are not outliers. The Carta data shows single-founder startup formation
has risen from 23.7% (2019) to 36.3% (2025). Y Combinator's spring 2025 batch
reported that 25% of teams had codebases that were 95% AI-generated.

The coordination cost that venture capital once financed is approaching zero.

When coordination is free, **organizational size becomes a liability**, not an
asset. A 10-person team with AI agents can outmaneuver a 1,000-person company
on most knowledge-work tasks — and carry none of the overhead that justifies
traditional VC economics.

---

## The Breakdown

As the new species emerges, the old infrastructure begins to fail:

- VC funds from the 2021 vintage are delivering historically poor DPI.
- Middle-tier VC firms ($100M–$500M) are unable to raise their next fund.
- The number of US IPOs per year has collapsed to a fraction of its 1990s median.
- The companies that *do* IPO now do so at a median age of 13–14 years — meaning
  all the value creation already happened privately.
- Secondary markets like Forge and EquityZen have grown to handle over $100 billion
  annually, displacing the IPO's historical function of providing liquidity.

The public market is becoming a **ceremony for retirees** — a stage on which
already-mature companies perform one final capital raise before settling into
dividend payments.

The new species does not need that ceremony.
But it still needs **something**.

---

## What It Needs

The new species needs:

1. **A listing venue** that does not punish smallness, does not require
   SOX-grade compliance for $5M-revenue companies, and does not treat employee
   count as a proxy for legitimacy.

2. **A trading layer** that does not rely on human attention spans, that
   processes information at the speed of agents, and that allows human
   investors to express *intent* rather than execute *trades*.

3. **A capital layer** that does not assume every good company must grow to
   10,000 employees, that tolerates a 10-person company staying at 10 people
   forever, and that rewards resilience over headcount.

This repository proposes one coherent answer: **TINY** (the listing venue) and
**AGORA** (the trading layer).

## Why We Are Writing This, Not Building It

We do not believe the author of a specification is always the right person to
implement it. History shows that the most durable protocols — TCP/IP, HTTP,
Bitcoin, Nostr — were seeded by authors who then stepped aside and let others
build.

This document is the seed.

Someone reading this already has the skills to implement a fragment of it.
Someone else has the regulatory contacts to open a jurisdictional pathway.
Someone else has the capital to fund the first prototype.

We are trying to make it easy for those three people to find each other.

---

## Why Japan

Japan occupies a unique position in this transition:

- Its **Growth market (東証グロース)** has, for twenty years, operated as the only
  major-economy listing venue that tolerates small companies. It has the
  institutional memory required to host a TINY-style market.
- Its **demographic collapse** (working-age population falling from 87M in 1995
  to a projected 59M by 2050) makes small-team economics an existential
  necessity, not a lifestyle preference.
- Its **regulatory culture** — cautious, but capable of decisive special-zone
  designations (as with the Fukuoka startup zone) — allows experimentation
  without national-scale risk.

Japan is not guaranteed to lead. But Japan is the jurisdiction most structurally
suited to host this transition if it chooses to.
